# Masked / somatised depression

> [!important] What this concept names
> A depressive presentation where the **predominant complaint is
> somatic** (chest tightness, sleep disturbance, fatigue) and / or
> **functional flatness** (half-volume, watching oneself work)
> rather than the prototypical sad / tearful affect. The phenomenon
> is well-described in primary care and in male-socialised
> patient cohorts where directly naming "depressed" is culturally
> unavailable, but the underlying syndrome meets standard depression
> criteria on instruments like the PHQ-9.

This is a **descriptive label**, not a separate DSM-5-TR diagnosis.
Clinicians use it to track the gap between *how the patient
spontaneously frames their distress* and *how the symptom set would
be coded on a standard instrument*. The gap matters because:

1. Patients who experience their distress somatically often see
   their PCP / GP for cardiac, GI, or pain work-ups before they see
   anyone equipped to treat depression.
2. Treatment plans that depend on the patient self-identifying as
   "depressed" hit a vocabulary wall that has nothing to do with
   willingness to engage.

## Phenomenological cluster

- **Somatic anchor** in chest, throat, neck/shoulder, or epigastrium
  — sustained, often with diurnal pattern.
- **Sleep disturbance** — often early-morning waking (terminal
  insomnia) rather than initial insomnia.
- **Anhedonia framed as flatness** — "everything's at half volume",
  "watching myself do it" — rather than as sadness or hopelessness.
- **Functional preservation at work** with **emotional withdrawal
  at home** — competence on visible tasks masks the depth of the
  withdrawal.
- **Limited self-labeling** — the patient resists or simply does
  not produce the word "depressed"; uses "tired", "off", "not
  myself" instead.

## How it differs from prototypical MDD presentation

| Axis | Prototypical MDD | Masked / somatised |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lead complaint | "I feel sad / hopeless" | "My chest is tight" / "I can't sleep" |
| Affect in room | Tearful, low | Constricted, flat-but-responsive |
| Self-frame | "I'm depressed" | "Something's off" |
| PHQ-9 score | 10-20 typical | 10-20 typical (same range) |
| First professional contact | Mental health | PCP, cardiology, sleep clinic |

The PHQ-9 score range is the same. The instruments capture both
presentations; the patient-side experience and route-into-care differ.

## How it appears in this material

Mark's presentation is a textbook example:

- **Lead complaint** is cardiac-sounding: chest tightness, "a hand
  closing around the bottom of my throat" ([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [12:08]).
- **First professional contact** was his PCP for cardiac work-up
  (normal EKG and stress test); the PHQ-9 was administered only
  because the cardiac work was negative.
- **Self-frame** explicitly resists the standard depression label:
  *"I'm not depressed in the I-want-to-disappear way. I'm depressed
  in the I-can't-feel-anything way"* ([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [12:08]).
- **Functional preservation at work, withdrawal at home** is the
  diurnal pattern: standup runs fine, two-people-on-couch evenings
  are non-watching ([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [03:47],
  [[2026-04-16-session-reyes]] [00:00]).
- **Predominant affect** is *flatness*, not sadness — the
  "watching myself" two-of-me phenomenology
  ([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [03:47],
  [[2026-04-23-psychiatry-han]] [08:22]).
- **PHQ-9 = 14** at both PCP and psychiatry consult — moderate
  range. The score does not distinguish "masked" from
  "prototypical" presentation; the *route* and the *self-frame* do.

## Treatment implications visible in this arc

- The route-into-care via PCP cardiac work-up is **typical** for
  masked presentations. Failing the PHQ-9 administration step at
  PCP would have left Mark in the somatic loop indefinitely. This
  is why primary-care PHQ-9 screening on negative cardiac work-ups
  is high-value.
- Treatment that **starts from the body and works back to the
  affect** (somatic anchoring, IFS protector-part work) is
  better-matched to this presentation than purely cognitive
  approaches. Dr. Reyes's intervention in session 2
  ([[2026-04-16-session-reyes]] [04:38]) lands precisely because
  it uses the chest band as the on-ramp rather than asking the
  client to talk about feelings he does not yet have words for.
- The **vocabulary expansion** itself is a clinical outcome.
  Mark's ability to *name* the foreman as a part, to *name*
  Sarah's reframe of "protecting yourself from being seen", to
  *name* his own choice not to start trazodone with a coherent
  rationale — those are markers of integration that wouldn't
  appear if the depressive picture were prototypically affective.

## Differential

- **vs. primary panic / anxiety disorder.** Chest tightness alone
  can be panic. The diurnal sustained pattern (not paroxysmal),
  the absence of fear-of-dying overlay, and the lack of acute
  panic episodes argue against panic disorder as the primary
  frame.
- **vs. alexithymia trait.** Alexithymia is a **trait** difficulty
  identifying / describing emotions; masked depression is a
  **state** presentation that can occur in alexithymic or
  non-alexithymic individuals. Mark's pre-loss baseline (per
  his report) included emotional warmth toward Theo and toward
  Sarah; his current limited vocabulary is state-bound to the
  grief presentation, not a lifelong trait.
- **vs. PTSD somatisation.** PTSD can present somatically. The
  PCL-5 = 28 (sub-threshold) here, with avoidance cluster
  prominent but no criterion-A trauma, points away from primary
  PTSD ([[2026-04-23-psychiatry-han]] [14:00]).
