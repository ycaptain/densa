## Narrative reconstruction (briefer than therapy, per psychiatry template)

The session opens with Han establishing the room's decision-
making style explicitly — collaborative, no first-visit
prescription default. This framing matters because Mark's
medication-reluctance has a coherent biographical basis
(paternal medication regimen) and a less articulated
psychological basis (the protector reading medication as
*overriding* the foreman's job). Han neutralises both by
treating the medication question as something to **discuss**,
not to *decide unilaterally*.

History-taking is brisk and structured: family psychiatric
history (maternal aunt, depression in 50s; paternal probable
PTSD un-diagnosed), substance use (2 beers / evening, monitoring
for creep), medical (borderline high BP, otherwise unremarkable),
sleep pattern (5h / night maintenance insomnia, 6/7 nights),
daytime functioning (low but preserved).

Safety check explicit and affirmative: no SI, no plans, no means;
the *"already kind of half gone"* framing is contrasted with
*"I want to disappear"* and identified as anhedonic rather than
suicidal.

Scales-pass: PHQ-9 = 14 (matches PCP), PCL-5 = 28 (Han notes
sub-threshold and avoidance-cluster dominant), GAD-7 = 9, DES-II
WNL. Han's running commentary while scoring is itself
clinically informative — he explicitly distinguishes a 14 in
bereavement context from a 14 in primary MDD, and notes that
two of the three highest PCL-5 items are in the avoidance
cluster (the maternal-call avoidance is exactly what those
items capture).

The medication conversation is the session's clinical centre.
Han structures it in **three explicit questions** ([22:30]):

1. **Is this PGD, MDD, or both?** Not PGD by timeframe
   (DSM-5-TR requires ≥12 months). Not primary MDD pending
   more time. Chart will read "bereavement-related depressive
   episode with complicated-grief features."
2. **Does medication help and for what?** Decoupled into the
   SSRI question (the whole depressive picture) and the
   trazodone question (sleep maintenance specifically).
3. **What's the follow-up cadence?**

The reasoning Han applies on the SSRI question is the most
clinically interesting move:

> *"Grief is informational — the feelings carry information
> about what mattered, what's unfinished, what to do next.
> SSRIs can be helpful when grief becomes a closed-loop trap,
> but you're not in a closed loop yet — you're in active
> processing."* ([[2026-04-23-psychiatry-han]] [22:30])

This is not a uniformly-held position in contemporary
psychiatry — Han is making a clinical judgment in light of
*this case's* active engagement with process work. The
decision is reasoned, not reflexive; the alternative reasoning
(SSRI initiation reduces overall symptom burden, which lets
process work proceed in better conditions) is acknowledged
elsewhere in the literature. Han's case-specific reasoning
turns on Mark's intact session-to-session engagement with
Reyes and on the absence of the closed-loop features (no
suicidality, no work decompensation, intact partner
relationship) that would push toward earlier pharmacology.

The trazodone question is decoupled because sleep deprivation
at 5h / night is materially contributing to the apparent
depressive load, and treating sleep *separately* lets each
decision rest on its own evidence. Trazodone 50mg PRN q.h.s.
is offered as the short-term reach (helps with maintenance
insomnia not just onset), paired with CBT-I as the medium-term
gold standard. Both are *held* — Mark gets two weeks to
discuss with Sarah and with Reyes before any prescription.

The session closes with the explicit cross-clinical boundary
statement at [33:18] — Han references the foreman work as a
*positive prognostic indicator* (system has parts willing to be
seen, not as "fortress-y" as PHQ-9 alone would suggest), but
*does not do process work* in the chair. Process material is
returned to the therapy channel.
