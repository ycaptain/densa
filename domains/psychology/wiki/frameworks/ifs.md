# Internal Family Systems (IFS)

> [!important] Framework one-liner
> An evidence-supported, non-pathologising parts-based psychotherapy
> developed by **Richard Schwartz** (PhD, 1995). Treats the psyche
> as a multiplicity of **parts** — sub-personalities with distinct
> roles, ages, and burdens — held within a *Self* characterised by
> calm, curiosity, compassion, courage, connectedness, clarity,
> creativity, and confidence ("the 8 Cs"). The therapeutic move is
> **not** to "fix" or "remove" parts but to access Self-energy, earn
> the trust of protector parts, and *unburden* exiled parts.

This page is a **compressed reference**, not a textbook. It captures
exactly what the worked-example arc uses, plus enough background
that a reader new to IFS can follow the analyses.

## Core model

### Parts categories

- **Self** — the seat of consciousness when no part is "blended"
  (in IFS terminology, a part is *blended* when it dominates
  attention and identity). Self-energy is the curative agent: when
  the Self is present, parts can be heard, and unburdening becomes
  possible. The 8 Cs are Self markers.
- **Protectors** — parts that work to prevent overwhelm.
  Two subtypes:
  - *Managers* — proactive protectors. Plan, control, achieve,
    caretake. Run daily life. Aim to prevent Exile activation.
  - *Firefighters* — reactive protectors. Activate when an Exile
    breaches awareness. Suppress overwhelm fast and crudely:
    substances, dissociation, rage, compulsive behaviour, food.
- **Exiles** — wounded / vulnerable parts holding the original
  burdens (shame, fear, abandonment, grief). Usually young.
  Protectors organise around keeping them out of awareness.

[[exiles-and-firefighters]] expands on this terminology with
worked examples from this arc.

### Clinical sequence (the "6 Fs")

A compressed version of the IFS protocol Schwartz uses:

1. **Find** the part (often via somatic signal — "where in your
   body do you feel that?").
2. **Focus** attention on it without forcing it to change.
3. **Flesh out** the part — its age, posture, what it wears,
   what it carries.
4. **Feel toward** the part (Self-energy check) — if the
   feeling-toward is not in the 8C range, *another protector* is
   in the way; loop back to step 1 with that part.
5. **beFriend** the part — let it tell its story, what it has
   been doing, what it is protecting.
6. **Fears** — ask the protector what it is afraid would happen
   if it stopped doing its job. This usually points to the Exile.

Unburdening Exiles is a separate, slower protocol that follows
once the protectors give permission.

### Non-pathologising stance

The IFS clinical stance is that **no part is bad**. Even the most
destructive Firefighter is doing a job — usually a job inherited
from an earlier developmental context where it was adaptive. The
clinical move is to **earn the protector's trust**, not override
it.

This stance is what makes IFS particularly well-matched to
clients with strong Manager-part dominance (high-functioning men
socialised in containment cultures; "I'm fine" presentations).
Approaches that treat the Manager as resistance / defence to be
broken through produce push-back; IFS treats the Manager as an
ally that has been carrying too much.

## Evidence base (brief)

- IFS has accumulated multiple RCTs in the last decade, including
  for **PTSD** (Hodgdon et al. 2022), **rheumatoid arthritis
  pain + depression** (Shadick et al. 2013), **depression**
  (numerous open trials).
- Designated *evidence-based* by SAMHSA's National Registry in
  2015; subsequently the registry was retired but the underlying
  outcome data remain.
- For **grief** specifically: less direct RCT evidence than for
  PTSD or pain; the rationale for IFS use in grief is mechanistic
  (it gives protectors a way to allow Exile contact without being
  overwhelmed) rather than effect-size-driven.

## When IFS is the right reach (in this arc)

The decision to introduce IFS framing in session 2 was driven by
**three signals**:

1. **Two-of-me phenomenology** in session 1
   ([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [03:47]) — already implicitly a
   parts-language description.
2. **Strong somatic anchor** that the client could attend to but
   not yet *name* — the chest band
   ([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [12:08]). IFS's body-first entry
   point matches this presentation.
3. **Containment-style baseline** with intact Self-capacity but
   over-deployed Manager — a presentation IFS is built for.

The corresponding signals against IFS as primary frame would be:
heavy Firefighter activity requiring stabilisation first; active
psychosis or thought-disorder where parts-language can scaffold
delusional material; or a client preference against parts-language
on conceptual grounds. None applied here.

- Reyes uses the 6F sequence implicitly:
  - **Find**: somatic attention to the chest band
    ([[2026-04-16-session-reyes]] [04:38]).

    words; Reyes endorses it without flattening it.
  - **Fears**: the protector's fear is "if the chest came open,
    the foreman couldn't run the job site"
    ([[2026-04-16-session-reyes]] [04:38]) — the Exile material
    underneath is the cottage-cheese grief.

## Limits / what IFS does not do in this arc

- IFS does not by itself produce the **information** that
  unburdens the guilt-charge — the mother's disclosure of the
  father's pre-death intent provides external information that
  the intrapsychic work *receives*. IFS's role is to make the
  receiver-side capacity available.
- IFS does not address **sleep maintenance** (the 3:40 a.m.
  waking) directly. Psychiatry handles that channel
  ([[medication-arc]]).
- IFS does not produce the **meaning-reconstruction** content
  ([[grief-as-love-transformed]]) — IFS unburdens, meaning-work
  rebuilds. The two are sequenced: integration follows
  unburdening.
