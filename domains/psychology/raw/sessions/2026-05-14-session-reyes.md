**[00:00]**

**Speaker 1:** Hi Mark. Come on in. How are you doing this morning?

**Speaker 2:** *(sets down coffee)* Different. I think. I'm not sure how to start.

**Speaker 1:** That's an interesting opener. "Different" — different from last session, or different from a baseline?

**Speaker 2:** Different from last session, which was already different from the one before that. So — different-different.

**Speaker 1:** Okay. Take your time. Where do you want to start?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, ~6 sec)* Okay. So Saturday — three days ago, May 10th — I called my mom. I called her, she didn't call me. And we talked for an hour and ten minutes. And about, I don't know, forty minutes in, I — I told her about the fight. The one with Dad. Right before he died. I told her about the fight and I told her that I'd been thinking about it constantly for two and a half months. And she — *(pause, eyes wet)* — she cried, but not like the cries I'd been avoiding. A different cry. And then she told me something I didn't know about him. And I — I think the shape of the whole thing changed. I don't know how else to say it. The shape changed.

**[02:48]**

**Speaker 1:** Take a breath. We have time. What did she tell you?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, ~10 sec)* She told me that Dad had — that the week before he died, he'd been talking about driving down to see me. Just me, not the family — just to come over and have a beer and watch a Sunday game. He'd told her he wanted to, like, smooth things over but didn't know how. He'd said — and these are her words, my mom's words — he'd said "I don't know how to do this kind of conversation. But it's been eating at me." That was, like, the Wednesday before. He died on the Wednesday after.

**Speaker 1:** *(quiet)*

**Speaker 2:** So for two and a half months I've been carrying around the idea that he died with no — that we'd both just kind of let the fight rot in the corner. And it turns out he wasn't letting it rot. He was just — he didn't know how to. He was working on it. And the way he was working on it was, he was going to drive three hours to drink a beer with me. Which is — *(short laugh)* — which is the most Bob Eldridge way of doing emotional repair, like, conceivable.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah.

**Speaker 2:** And my mom — she said she'd been wondering whether to tell me. She wasn't sure if it would make it better or worse. And the reason she finally told me was because I'd called *her*, instead of waiting for her to call me, and she thought maybe that meant I was, like, ready.

**Speaker 1:** Was she right?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* I think so. Yeah. I don't — I'm not "fine" now. The 3:40 thing is still happening. But the regret-thing has — moved. The story isn't "we left it broken." The story is "we were both trying, in our own dumb ways, and the world didn't give us the time." Which is — it's still sad, it's maybe sadder in a way, but it's a different sad. It's not a *guilty* sad. Like the foreman has been carrying around this — this charge of *neglect* this whole time. And he's been justified in carrying it, because the story has been "you neglected to fix this with him." And now the story is "you and him were both walking toward each other and the road ended early." The foreman set that one down on Saturday. I could feel him set it down.

**[06:30]**

**Speaker 1:** Stay with what just happened in your body, saying that.

**Speaker 2:** *(closes eyes, pauses, ~12 sec)* The chest — the chest is the loosest it's been in months. The hand is still there, but it's, like, hands-on-knees, taking a break, not chest-pressing. It's like the foreman went and sat on a bucket.

**Speaker 1:** That's an image. The foreman went and sat on a bucket.

**Speaker 2:** *(laughs, opens eyes)* Yeah. That's exactly the image.

**Speaker 1:** Good. Let it be there for a minute.

**Speaker 1:** Let me ask — what made you call her on Saturday rather than wait for her call? Because that's the variable that changed, right? The conversation could have happened months ago, but it required you initiating. What shifted?

**Speaker 2:** *(thinks)* So Saturday morning I was up at 5, as usual. And I was making coffee, and Theo had left this — he's seven, he draws constantly — he'd left this drawing on the kitchen table the night before. And I asked Sarah later, "who's the third person in Theo's drawing?" and Sarah said, "oh, that's your dad. He draws your dad in those a lot lately." And I — *(pause)* — I didn't know that. I didn't know my seven-year-old was drawing my dad into the family pictures. And I, I was standing in the kitchen at 5:30 a.m. and I thought, like, my dad is — my dad is *part of* what I'm passing to Theo, and right now I'm passing to Theo the version where you don't talk to your mom on Saturdays. **Speaker 1:** Hm.

**Speaker 2:** And, I mean — I called her without rehearsing it, which is — I always rehearse. I always plan. I just hit dial. So that was — the foreman didn't get a chance to talk me out of it. Like, the same part of me that's been holding the chest down said, "okay, time to do this." Which is — that's different from how it was a month ago.

**[12:14]**

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. That's actually a really important distinction. Tell me more — what changed for the foreman, you think?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* I think — okay, so the foreman's job has always been "keep Mark running." And for two months his idea of "keep Mark running" has been "don't pick up phone calls from Mom because they break the system." But somewhere in the last few weeks — I think starting with the cottage-cheese session, honestly — the foreman has been getting different information. Like, he's getting evidence that letting a little bit of grief through, in the right room, doesn't break the system. The session with you on the 30th — when we did that thing with the chair, when I, like, talked to the foreman — that was, like, the foreman getting evidence that he could be talked to without being fired. And then the Han session on the 23rd, where Han basically said, "I see what you're doing, I'm not going to medicate over it, I think the therapy work is the right thing" — like, the foreman heard that. He heard a doctor in a white coat say, "the way you're working is correct." That was huge for him.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. That tracks.

**Speaker 2:** So I think Saturday morning at 5:30 in the kitchen, with Theo's drawing, the foreman had enough evidence to make a different call. He still made the call — that's important to me, by the way. It wasn't "the foreman stepped aside and a younger part picked up the phone." The foreman dialled. He just dialled with new information.

**Speaker 1:** That's exactly right. That's how this work actually goes. The protector parts don't get fired. They get re-deployed.

**[16:42]**

**Speaker 1:** Tell me about Sarah this week.

**Speaker 2:** Okay. So Saturday, after I got off the phone with Mom, I went to find Sarah. She was in the laundry room folding sheets. And I — I told her about the conversation. The whole thing. Including the drawing. And she — she started crying, which Sarah doesn't really do, and she said, "you haven't told me anything that real in three months." And I — I think I'd been telling myself I was protecting her from how bad it was. And what she said back to me was, "you weren't protecting me, you were protecting yourself from having to be seen by me." Which — okay, that one hit. *(pause)* And she was right.

**Speaker 1:** How did you receive that, in the moment? Because that's a sharp sentence, even if it's affectionate.

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* I think — I think a month ago that sentence would have shut me down. I would have, like, nodded, and said "yeah you're right," and then gone outside for forty minutes. Saturday I — I just stayed. I stayed in the laundry room and we both cried for a minute and then I helped her fold the sheets. And — that was — that was a whole different day than I've had in a while. The thing I want to flag, though, is that Sunday and Monday were *bad*. Sunday I was a zombie. Monday I cried in the bathroom at work for like four minutes between meetings. So this isn't, like, a breakthrough that makes it all easier. If anything Sunday and Monday were like the foreman saying, "okay if we're going to do this, we're going to *do* this, and you're going to feel the rest of it now."

**Speaker 1:** That's how it works. Real shifts make things, on average, more painful in the short run, not less. The integration is the relief — and that takes weeks, not days. What you described — picking up the phone, telling Sarah, surviving Sunday — that's the work. Sunday and Monday were not a setback. They were Sunday and Monday.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** And — physically? The 3:40?

**Speaker 2:** Still happening. Maybe — maybe a hair softer? Like maybe I'm getting back to sleep some nights. But it's — moving a little.

**Speaker 1:** Good. You see Dr. Han on the 21st — next Thursday, right?

**Speaker 2:** Yeah, the 21st. Original plan was trazodone-decision then. Honestly I think — I think I'd want to hold off on the trazodone too, at this point. **Speaker 1:** That's your call to make with Han. He'll respect it. Tell him about Saturday and tell him about Sarah; he'll have the data he needs.

**[24:00]**

**Speaker 1:** I want to come back to the cottage cheese moment, because I said I would, and I want to come back to it now that something has actually shifted in the system.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Three weeks ago, when we were sitting here, the cottage cheese was unbearable. It was the place the foreman was holding the hardest down. And what we did then was open it for sixty seconds and close it. Where is the cottage cheese now?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, ~15 sec)* I can — I can look at it. I can — I can picture his kitchen. The fridge, the cottage cheese tub, the daisies-pattern dishtowel he had. I can — I can be in the kitchen without — without the hand closing. There's something — there's something *tender* about the tub of cottage cheese now instead of *unbearable*. Like — it's just a tub of cottage cheese. And he liked it. And he ate it the morning he died. And — that's a part of him that gets to be remembered with a little bit of *fondness* now. Not just with — *(pause)* — not just with grief.

**Speaker 1:** What lets it be tender now?

**Speaker 2:** *(thinks)* I think — I think the foreman is letting it be tender because he's not having to hold the *guilt* down anymore. The guilt was the thing that made the cottage cheese unbearable, not the grief. The grief by itself is, like — yeah, it's sad. It's deeply sad. It hurts. But it's *clean*. The guilt was making it dirty. It was — the cottage cheese wasn't a tub of cottage cheese, it was *evidence that I'd let my dad die without making it right.* And now that I know he was working on it from his end, it goes back to being a tub of cottage cheese that my dad liked. Which is just — sad. Just sad.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. Grief is love with no place to go. Without the guilt-distortion, it's still grief — it'll still hurt for a long time — but it has more shape than it had. It can integrate.

**Speaker 2:** *(quiet)* "Love with no place to go." Yeah.

**Speaker 1:** That's not mine. Jamie Anderson said it. But it shows up in the literature, in different forms — Robert Neimeyer's meaning-reconstruction work, that's the larger frame. Grief isn't a problem to be solved. It's love that has to find a new shape because the relationship it was directed at has changed form.

**[30:08]**

**Speaker 1:** Where do we go from here?

**Speaker 2:** I — I think a few things. One — I want to keep doing this. I don't think I'm "done." **Speaker 1:** Yeah. That's a real piece of meaning-making, by the way. What you just described — taking what you got from your dad, naming what was missing without contempt, and choosing to do it one notch better with Theo — that's classic meaning-reconstruction. That's the work. That's how grief becomes part of who you are instead of a thing that happened to you.

**Speaker 2:** Yeah.

**Speaker 1:** Practical for between now and next session — two weeks? Three?

**Speaker 2:** Two weeks. The 28th. I have a work thing the week of the 4th.

Writing him a letter and not sending it. Talking to him out loud in the truck. There's no "right" version. The function is: give the conversation a place to happen now, even though he's not there to have it with. People sometimes call this a *continuing bond*. It's a real piece of grief integration; it has empirical support.
