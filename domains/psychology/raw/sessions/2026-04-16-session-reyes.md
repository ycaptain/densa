# Session — Mark Eldridge & Dr. Elena Reyes, LCSW

Date: 2026-04-16
Mode: in-person
Duration: 50 min
Session number: 2

Speaker 1 = Dr. Elena Reyes (therapist)
Speaker 2 = Mark Eldridge (client, self)

---

**[00:00]**

**Speaker 1:** Hi Mark. Come in. Coffee? I just made a fresh pot — selfishly, I needed it.

**Speaker 2:** I'm okay, thanks. I drank too much already at standup.

**Speaker 1:** Fair. So — how was the week? Before we start anywhere structured, take a minute. Where are you starting today?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, exhales slowly)* Um. The week was — same. The same. The 3:40 thing is exactly the same. Work was fine, work was busy actually, we shipped a big release on Tuesday. Mom called twice; I picked up once on Saturday for about twenty minutes, didn't pick up Wednesday. Sarah and I had a tough Wednesday night — not a fight, just, um, distance. Sat on the couch watching TV and we weren't actually watching it, we were both somewhere else. That was bad.

**Speaker 1:** How did you know Wednesday was bad?

**Speaker 2:** I think because Thursday morning I — I woke up early as usual, but instead of just lying there I got up at like 4:30, made coffee, and just sat at the kitchen table doing nothing. For an hour. And I think that scared me a little, because usually I at least pretend to be useful — answer Slack, read the news. Thursday I didn't do anything. I just sat.

**Speaker 1:** What was happening in your body at the kitchen table?

**Speaker 2:** Same as before — *(touches upper chest, just below the collarbone)* — tight. Right here. Maybe a little lower than last week, but mostly the same band.

**Speaker 1:** Did you write any of the sentences down? I asked last week — notice when you think about calling your mom, write a sentence if you can.

**Speaker 2:** *(half-laughs)* Yeah, I tried. So Saturday morning before I called her, I sat down for about a minute and tried to do the thing. And the sentence I wrote was — let me — *(pulls out phone, reads)* — "The phone is heavier than the phone." That's what I wrote. Which is, like, gibberish, but —

**Speaker 1:** It's not gibberish.

**Speaker 2:** No?

**Speaker 1:** No. Say it again.

**Speaker 2:** *(quietly)* The phone is heavier than the phone.

**[04:38]**

**Speaker 1:** What do you notice when you say it out loud?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* I notice my arm — like, my actual arm — feels heavier. And the thing in my chest, like, fluttered a second. And then went back to being a band.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. That sentence is a body sentence. The first "phone" is the physical phone. The second "phone" is the call you're about to be on, which is much heavier than its physical weight. Your body knows the second one. It's already bracing.

**Speaker 2:** Huh.

**Speaker 1:** Can I try something a little different today? I want to spend some time on what's in your body, not just what's in your head. We can absolutely keep talking the way we've been talking, but I think there's a part of this that — the regular conversation isn't going to reach it. Would you be willing to try?

**Speaker 2:** Sure. I mean, yes. I don't really know how that works but yes.

**Speaker 1:** Okay. So I'm going to ask you to close your eyes — or not, you can keep them open and look at the rug if that's more comfortable — and just put your attention on that band in your chest. Don't try to change it. Don't try to relax. Just — be curious about it. Like you're looking at a small animal you've never seen before.

**Speaker 2:** Okay. *(closes eyes after a few seconds, breathes)*

**Speaker 1:** What's it doing right now?

**Speaker 2:** It's, um. It's there. It's like — it's not a band, actually, when I look at it. It's more like — like someone's hand. Like, a — a hand of someone strong who's holding my chest down, gently but firmly. Not crushing. Just — holding it down.

**Speaker 1:** Holding it down so it doesn't —?

**Speaker 2:** *(longer pause, ~8 sec)* So it doesn't blow open. Like — if the hand wasn't there, my chest would just — *(makes a brief outward gesture)*.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. And the hand — does the hand have any sense of who it is, or where it's from, or what it's protecting you from?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* That's — that's a weird question.

**Speaker 1:** I know. Stay with it.

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, ~15 sec)* It's — I keep wanting to say the hand is my dad's hand. But it's not, it doesn't feel like Dad. It feels like — okay this is going to sound bananas — it feels like a version of *me*. An older version of me. Like — like a foreman. The kind of guy who runs a job site. Sleeves rolled up, doesn't say much. I want to call it — I don't know, like —

**Speaker 1:** Don't name it yet. Just — does it have an attitude? A posture? A job?

**Speaker 2:** Its job is to keep me running. To get me through the day. Through standup, through dinner, through bedtime with Theo. It's been doing the job since — *(pause)* — I don't know. A long time. Since before Dad died. Maybe since I was — I don't know. Since I was a kid. Since I was twelve maybe. Around when my parents started having trouble.

**Speaker 1:** And what does it want for you?

**Speaker 2:** It wants me to be okay. It wants me to be okay and to not — to not be the kind of guy who falls apart. That's its whole — that's its whole brief. Don't be the guy who falls apart. Get up. Go to work. Do the job. Pay the mortgage. Show up for Theo. Be the rock.

**Speaker 1:** And the chest band — the hand holding your chest down — that's how it does the job.

**Speaker 2:** *(quietly)* Yeah. *(longer pause)* Yeah, that's how it does the job. Because if the chest came open, the foreman couldn't run the job site. It's a — it's a *containment* thing. *(opens eyes)* Was that the word you used last week?

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. I used it last week. It's good that it came back to you this way. Stay with the feeling — don't lose it just because we put a word on it.

**[14:55]**

**Speaker 1:** Mark, what I just heard you describe is what some clinicians call a "protector part." It's a part of you with a job. It's not all of you. It's not even most of you. But it's been on shift for a long time, and right now it's working overtime because what's coming up under the hand is heavier than anything it's had to hold before.

**Speaker 2:** I'm — I don't — *(pause)* — I don't want to make this into some, like, multiple-personalities thing.

**Speaker 1:** It's not. Everyone has parts. The stoic foreman who runs the day, the kid who laughs at cottage cheese, the husband who can't quite tell Sarah how he is — those aren't symptoms. They're just different layers of you, with different ages and different jobs. The frame I'm borrowing from is called IFS, Internal Family Systems — Richard Schwartz. I'll send you a one-pager if you want. The reason I want to use it is, it lets us talk *to* the foreman instead of trying to argue *with* him.

**Speaker 2:** Okay. Yeah, send the one-pager.

**Speaker 1:** And — one more thing about the foreman. He's not the enemy. He saved you. He probably saved you at twelve. He's saving you right now, frankly — he's the reason you're at work, the reason Theo has dinner, the reason Sarah's not single-handedly running the house. If we just try to make him go away, two things will happen. One, he won't. Two, even trying would be a betrayal of someone who's done a hard job for a long time. So our move is not to fire him. Our move is, eventually, to thank him and let him take breaks. Right now he doesn't trust anyone else to cover the shift.

**Speaker 2:** *(quiet)* No. He doesn't.

**[18:01]**

**Speaker 1:** Let's try something else. Stay in your body for a second. The foreman is holding your chest. Underneath the foreman's hand — what's there?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, ~10 sec)* I don't — I'm not sure I want to look.

**Speaker 1:** Don't look hard. Glance. What do you glimpse?

**Speaker 2:** *(longer pause, voice quieter)* It's — it's the cottage cheese thing.

**Speaker 1:** Tell me.

**Speaker 2:** It's — I'm in his apartment, his kitchen, and I'm looking at this half-empty tub of cottage cheese, and the thing under the foreman's hand is — *(voice tightens)* — it's the fact that he was eating that yesterday, like just yesterday, he stood at this counter and he ate cottage cheese with a spoon out of the tub like he always did, and now he isn't, and he isn't going to, and I'll never see him stand in this kitchen again, and the cottage cheese is just *(pause)* — it doesn't know. The cottage cheese just sits there.

**Speaker 1:** *(quiet, doesn't move)*

**Speaker 2:** *(eyes wet but not crying)* And the foreman, like — the foreman caught it. The first time, in the apartment, he made me laugh at it, because laughing was a way to grieve without actually grieving. And then he drove me home. And he's been holding it down since.

**Speaker 1:** Stay with the cottage cheese for a few breaths. Don't push it down, don't push it up. Just — let it sit. It's allowed to sit.

**Speaker 2:** *(breathes for about a minute, doesn't speak)*

**Speaker 1:** What's the chest doing?

**Speaker 2:** It's — softer. The hand is still there, but the hand is — looser. Like, the hand knows we're looking and it's allowing it for a minute.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. That's good. That's enough for today on this. We're not going to push past it.

**[26:30]**

**Speaker 1:** Let me step back. What just happened, in clinician language, is you and I located a piece of grief that the daily-running part of you has been holding away from awareness, and we let it surface for about a minute in a room that's safe enough to let it surface. That's a real piece of work. It doesn't fix anything, but it changes what's possible. The foreman now has a tiny bit more evidence that he doesn't have to do the job alone.

**Speaker 2:** *(nods slowly)*

**Speaker 1:** I want to be careful between sessions. I don't want you sitting at your kitchen table at 4:30 a.m. trying to recreate this on your own — that's not how it works, and you don't have a container for it there. What I'd suggest is — if the cottage cheese thing surfaces during the week, you let it surface for a few seconds, you tell yourself "I noticed you, I'll come back to you on Thursday," and you let the foreman do his job for now. Treat the foreman with respect. Don't fight him.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Next Thursday is the 23rd — actually no, that's your psychiatry appointment with Dr. Han. So we're skipping a week and meeting on the 30th. How are you feeling about Dr. Han?

**Speaker 2:** Honestly? I don't really want to be on medication. My dad was on like four different things at the end and he hated it, he stopped taking the blood pressure stuff at some point I think. So I have a — yeah, I have a thing about medication. But Sarah wants me to go, and you said it was a good idea, and the sleep thing is — I am tired. I am very tired.

**Speaker 1:** Couple things. One — Dr. Han knows medication is a sensitive topic for a lot of people, including grieving people who've watched a parent manage a regimen. He won't push. Two — there's a real conversation to be had about whether starting an SSRI in week eight of grief is the right call or the wrong call. There's an argument either way. He'll have a view; my view, for what it's worth, is that there's a sleep-piece question that's almost independent of the depression-medication question. Bring both. Three — and this matters — tell him about what we did today. He'll want to know the protector frame is in play. It changes what he'd recommend if he knew you were already doing process work.

**Speaker 2:** Okay. I can do that.

**Speaker 1:** And — the call to your mom this past Saturday. You picked up. How was it?

**Speaker 2:** It was — it was twenty minutes of her crying for the first eight and then talking about her sister's kids for twelve. I held it. I got off the phone, I went outside, I sat on the back porch for an hour. Sarah brought me a beer at some point. I didn't say much. But I held it.

**Speaker 1:** That's something. That's the foreman doing his job *and* letting twenty minutes through. That's not nothing.

**Speaker 2:** Doesn't feel like much.

**Speaker 1:** It rarely does in week three. Trust me on this one.

**[36:42]**

**Speaker 1:** Two practical things before we close. One — I notice you said "I don't quite have the words" with Sarah, and the same general shape comes up with your mom. The IFS frame may help here too: the foreman doesn't have words because the foreman's job isn't to speak feelings. There's another part of you that has the words — maybe younger, maybe more vulnerable. We'll meet him eventually. Two — Dr. Han is next Thursday. Our next is the 30th. Between now and the 30th, if anything sharp happens — the 3:40 wakings get worse, an active wish to disappear, anything you can't sit with — call my service, they'll page me. I mean it.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Anything you want to say before we close?

**Speaker 2:** Just — thank you. That was — I wasn't expecting that today.

**Speaker 1:** You did the work. I just held the room. See you on the 30th.
