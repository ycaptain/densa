## 1 Introduction

As technology continues to reshape our lives, the emergence of
generative artificial intelligence (AI) is having an increasingly
important impact on education. ChatGPT — a text-generative chatbot
that can simulate conversations with human users — was launched by
OpenAI in 2022 (OpenAI, 2025), and it has gained considerable
attention for its potential to transform all aspects of teaching and
learning (Tate et al., 2023; Williams, 2023).

The authors use "generative AI" rather than the broader "AI" to
emphasize their focus on large language models such as ChatGPT.
ChatGPT is a type of generative AI designed as a chatbot that can
generate and understand text in a way that closely mirrors human
language.

Concerns raised by educators include: eroding academic integrity and
ethics (Siegle, 2023), the spread of misinformation (Cooper, 2023),
adoption of misleading information (Kılınç, 2023), and the loss of
human values and skills (Rahman & Watanobe, 2023).

Conversely, ChatGPT's potential contributions to education include
personalized learning experiences (Chan & Hu, 2023), generating
lesson ideas (Siegle, 2023), summarizing complex concepts (Tlili et
al., 2023), facilitating student-teacher communication (Kılınç,
2023), and supporting language acquisition (Yan, 2023). Much like
the introduction of calculators and the early Google search engine,
ChatGPT is seen as a double-edged sword — both a threat to human
cognitive development and a tool for educational innovation.

This review draws on **sociocultural theory, self-determination
theory, accessibility / personalized-differentiated learning, and
critical AI literacy** to interpret findings. The four research
questions are:

1. What are the central purposes of empirical research studies
   focused on ChatGPT in educational contexts?
2. What practices integrating ChatGPT in educational contexts have
   been identified through empirical research?
3. What challenges has empirical research identified when using
   ChatGPT in educational contexts?
4. What research methods have been used in empirical studies of
   ChatGPT in educational contexts?

## 2 Theoretical and Conceptual Background

### 2.1 Sociocultural Theory

Vygotsky's (1978) "zone of proximal development" (ZPD) names the
knowledge and skills students can learn given appropriate support
from cognitive tools and/or more knowledgeable others. Sociocultural
theory focuses on tools or technologies which **mediate human
thought and action**. From this perspective, ChatGPT can mediate
students' learning by providing technology-enhanced learning
opportunities (Ormrod, 2011; Thorne, 2024).

### 2.2 Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

SDT (Deci & Ryan, 2012; Ryan & Deci, 2000) posits three basic
psychological needs: **autonomy, competence, relatedness**. A
subtheory, *cognitive evaluation theory* (CET), focuses on the
interconnection of autonomy and competence. Intrinsic motivation
is thwarted when feedback undermines competence or rewards seem
outside one's control; it is enhanced by positive feedback that
supports autonomous goals.

### 2.3 Accessibility and Personalized/Differentiated Learning

The **Universal Design for Learning (UDL)** framework (Meyer et al.,
2014) has three standards for accessible learning environments:

1. Multiple opportunities for student **engagement**.
2. Multiple opportunities for **representation** (instructional
   tools).
3. Multiple opportunities for students to **demonstrate learning**.

Personalized, differentiated, and individualized learning are
incrementally more adaptive: all three permit pacing changes; only
differentiated and personalized permit method changes; only
personalized tailors instruction to student interests (U.S.
Department of Education, 2010).

### 2.4 Critical AI Literacy

Mills et al. (2023) identify three dimensions of critical AI
literacy: (1) developing domain-specific critical thinking skills
concerning AI-generated information, (2) questioning whether AI is
reliable and/or possibly harmful, and (3) promoting critical
pedagogies that encourage students to question the relationship
between power, inequality, and AI use.

Thomas and Lok (2015) frame critical thinking with three
inter-related attributes: disposition, knowledge, and skills.

### 2.5 Current Study

The four frameworks (sociocultural theory, SDT, accessibility/UDL,
critical AI literacy) are treated as **complementary and
interrelated lenses**.

## 3 Methodology

The review uses **qualitative content analysis** (Schreier, 2012),
which involves three phases: (1) examining and identifying relevant
studies, (2) developing a coding framework, and (3) identifying
common themes.

### 3.1 Phase 1 — Identifying Relevant Studies

- **Window:** January 2023 – December 2024.
- **Scope:** K-12 and higher education.
- **Sources:** ERIC database (primary) and Google Scholar
  (supplementary), plus purposeful search of seven leading
  technology / education journals: *Computers and Education*,
  *Journal of Educational Computing Research*, *The Internet and
  Higher Education*, *Journal of Computers in Education*, *Journal
  of Computing in Higher Education*, *International Journal of
  Educational Research*, and *Journal of Educational Research*. The
  journal search used the term "ChatGPT and education".
- **Initial pool:** 112 articles.
- **Exclusions (n = 60):** duplicates (2), no full text (2), non-
  empirical (41), not peer-reviewed (0), not explicitly focused on
  ChatGPT in education (15).
- **Final corpus:** **52 empirical articles**.

### 3.2 Phases 2 & 3 — Coding Framework and Themes

Two-team iterative coding:

- **Team A:** language and literacy studies (ELL, feedback, writing).
- **Team B:** STEM and broader educational contexts (personalised
  learning, accessibility, academic integrity).

Each team independently coded a subset; teams convened to compare
decisions, resolve discrepancies, and refine codes until consensus.
Peer debriefing and audit-trail maintenance supported trustworthiness.

Each article was coded on: (a) purpose statement; (b) participants
/ sample size / context / curriculum; (c) methodology, design, data
collection, analysis; (d) findings.

### 4.2 Practices — Enhancing Educational Practice Through ChatGPT

Four themes emerged.

#### 4.2.1 Enhancing Access and Understanding (n = 14)

The **most discussed** affordance: ChatGPT effectively summarizes
and presents complex topics so students can more readily access new
material (Aktay et al. 2023; Çelik et al. 2024; Chan & Hu 2023; …).

#### 4.2.2 Developing Curricula, Lesson Plans, and Assessments (n = 11)

Teachers can use ChatGPT as a teaching assistant — e.g., Jeon and
Lee (2023) found Korean elementary teachers valued ChatGPT's
capacity to develop content and assess work; Lee and Zhai (2024)
found pre-service teachers used it to design differentiated
instruction.

#### 4.2.3 Personalized, Engaging, and Efficient Learning

| Sub-theme                         | n |
| --------------------------------- | - |
| Personalized learning             | 9 |
| Conversational engagement         | 9 |
| Efficiency (time savings)         | 6 |
| Learner autonomy / self-directed  | 5 |

Personalized: Chan & Hu (2023); Jauhiainen & Guerra (2023);
Stojanov (2023). Engagement: Aktay et al. (2023); C. Liu et al.
(2024a). Efficiency: Ngo (2023); Mogavi et al. (2024). Autonomy:
Kakhki et al. (2024); Stojanov (2023); Kılınç (2023).

#### 4.2.4 Supporting English Learners and Teachers (n = 10)

ChatGPT provides targeted feedback on EFL writing (Escalante et al.
2023; Tseng & Lin 2024; Yan 2023; …). Four studies compared
ChatGPT's feedback to human tutors' feedback. **Three found ChatGPT
needed improved consistency** (Escalante et al. 2023; Evmenova et
al. 2024; Shabara et al. 2024). Guo and Wang (2024) noted ChatGPT
provides more extensive, directive feedback that supplements human
tutor feedback.

### 4.3 The Challenges of ChatGPT

Four themes.

#### 4.3.1 Harming Academic Integrity (n = 14)

The most-cited challenge. Students could over-rely on ChatGPT to
plagiarize (Chaudhry et al. 2023; Cong-Lem et al. 2024; Kakhki &
Gendron 2024; …). Current detection programs (and humans) struggle
to identify AI-produced text (Alexander et al. 2023; Sullivan et al.
2023; Yeadon et al. 2023). Perkins et al. (2024) found Turnitin was
generally effective, with a discrepancy between software and human
detection.

Counter-view: not all researchers agree the plagiarism risk is
significant. Gregorcic and Pendrill (2023) noted ChatGPT's responses
contained incorrect and contradictory information; Kortemeyer
(2023) found ChatGPT barely passes an introductory physics course;
Dahlkemper et al. (2023) found students rated human responses more
accurate than ChatGPT's.

#### 4.3.2 Producing Inaccurate, Biased, or Made-Up Information (n = 13)

C. Liu et al. (2024a); Gregorcic & Pendrill (2023); Kortemeyer (2023);
Lee & Zhai (2024); Tlili et al. (2023); …. Biased content favouring
some perspectives / identities (Aktay et al. 2023; Cooper 2023; …).
Cultural insensitivity (Kılınç 2023). Inaccuracies compounded by
ChatGPT not always providing references (Cooper 2023; Ngo 2023). One
positive note: ChatGPT-4 and Bing AI provide references (M. Liu et
al. 2024b).

#### 4.3.3 Providing Inaccurate Feedback (n = 6)

ChatGPT's potential for inaccurate feedback on student assessments
and teacher curriculum planning (Escalante et al. 2023; Jacob et al.
2024; Kılınç 2023; Shabara et al. 2024; Stojanov 2023; Tseng & Lin
2024). Researchers suggest using ChatGPT as a **tutoring tool rather
than for assessment** (Jacob et al. 2024; Stojanov 2023; Tseng &
Lin 2024); as a **starting point for exploration**, not a single
information source (Lee & Zhai 2024).

#### 4.3.4 Undermining Students' Critical Thinking Skills

Tension: some researchers suggest ChatGPT can foster critical
thinking (Bitzenbauer 2023; Zhu et al. 2023); others are concerned
ChatGPT has limited critical thinking (Zhu et al. 2023) and may
undermine students' critical thinking (Chan & Hu 2023; Rahman &
Watanobe 2023; Tlili et al. 2023). Over-reliance is the central
mechanism flagged.

## 5 Discussion

Four key insights on integration of generative AI in education:

1. Promoting personalized learning and learner autonomy.
2. Promoting equitable learning and accessibility.
3. Fostering pedagogical innovation through creative curriculum,
   lesson planning, and assessment design.
4. Promoting critical AI literacy and academic integrity.

### 5.1 Promoting Personalized Learning and Learner Autonomy

Students' sense of autonomy contributes to academic motivation
(Deci & Ryan, 2012). Jauhiainen and Guerra (2023) found generative
AI can successfully create personalized social-studies and history
lessons for 4th–6th graders; self-reported learning correlated with
lesson interest. University students reported ChatGPT-generated
personalized feedback increased their autonomy (Mahapatra, 2024).

### 5.2 Promoting Equitable Learning and Accessibility

**Equality vs. equity (Minow, 2021):** equality provides equal
resources regardless of individual needs; equity personalizes the
learning experience for each student.

UDL principles promote personalized learning, a strength of
generative AI. By alleviating barriers — language proficiency,
disabilities — ChatGPT can expand customized learning supports
(Escalante et al. 2023; Evmenova et al. 2024; Jeon & Lee 2023). It
can break down complex concepts (Aktay et al. 2023) and simplify
texts (Celik et al. 2024). M. Liu et al. (2024b) demonstrated
multimodal writing support (consistent with UDL principle 3).

> However, although ChatGPT promotes accessibility, it is crucial
> that it does not unintentionally exacerbate existing inequities.
> Users must pay for the unlimited-prompt version, plus internet
> connectivity and a device. **These economic barriers may limit
> some students' access and further exacerbate equity concerns.**
> One solution: increased technology and AI-centered funding for
> under-resourced schools.

### 5.3 Fostering Pedagogical Innovation

Two mechanisms:

1. **Development of learning resources.** Teachers can use ChatGPT
   to design formative assessments (Lee & Zhai 2024), customize
   materials (Jeon & Lee 2023), develop interactive activities, and
   auto-grade writing with targeted insights (Mizumoto & Eguchi
   2023). Multimodal extensions (DALL-E for images, SORA for video)
   support authentic learning scenarios (He et al. 2024).
2. **Workload redistribution toward higher-order tasks.** ChatGPT
   completes routine tasks (e.g., weekly vocabulary lists in Mena
   Octavio et al. 2024), freeing teacher time for creative
   activities and out-of-classroom experiences. Real-time
   personalized feedback (Nikolic et al. 2023). Guo and Wang (2024)
   found ChatGPT provided more extensive, directive feedback than
   teachers for EFL students; teachers offered more informative
   feedback with clarifying questions.

**Limits of AI feedback vs. human:** lack of empathy (Kılınç 2023);
cultural insensitivity (Mohamed 2024). Escalante et al. (2023)
found undergraduates were evenly split between AI vs. human tutor
preference. **Per SDT, relatedness is a key motivational component
— the human connection must not be missing.**

### 5.4 Promoting Critical AI Literacy and Academic Integrity

#### 5.4.1 Accuracy, Bias, and Use of AI

ChatGPT may produce **inaccurate, misleading, contradictory
information** (C. Liu et al. 2024a; Gregorcic & Pendrill 2023;
Kortemeyer 2023; Lee & Zhai 2024; Punar & Yangin 2024; Stojanov
2023; Zhu et al. 2023). **Bias** based on training data (Huallpa et
al. 2023; Taktak et al. 2024) — dominant perspectives over
marginalized voices.

**Over-reliance leading to plagiarism** (Chaudhry et al. 2023;
Cong-Lem et al. 2024; Kakhki & Gendron 2024) and harm to motivation
(Mahapatra 2024). AI detection tools (Grammarly, QuillBot, ZeroGPT,
Scribbr, Turnitin) provide partial help. Perkins et al. (2024)
found Turnitin generally detects AI content but is limited on
paraphrased content. Chaudhry et al. (2023) found detection tools
were not reliably accurate.

#### 5.4.2 Addressing Concerns

Developing **critical AI literacy** (Mills et al. 2023) is integral
to ethical use. The curriculum should provide AI-related knowledge
and skills to (a) critically examine reliability and accuracy of AI
output and (b) interrogate AI's role in current power structures.

Recommended pedagogical moves:

- **Treat ChatGPT as an advisory tool** — initial resource for
  information gathering and idea brainstorming (Barrett & Pack 2023;
  Ngo 2023; Stojanov 2023).
- **Guidance-based ChatGPT-assisted Learning Aid (GCLA)** — Lee et
  al. (2024) tool engaging students in critical thinking *before*
  receiving ChatGPT responses; promotes independent problem solving
  via hints.
- **Critical examination activities:** fact-checking exercises
  (Huang et al. 2023), identifying gaps in AI content (Huang et al.
  2023), reflective "how / why" questioning (Wu 2024).
- **Pack and Maloney (2023) four-step approach:** (1) assign role to
  ChatGPT (e.g., 5th-grade science teacher), (2) assign purpose,
  (3) specify bounds (suggest vs. modify), (4) refine output.

## 6 Conclusion

The review highlights ChatGPT's promise (autonomy, competence via
personalized learning experiences that promote equitable learning
and accessibility) and pertinent concerns (academic integrity,
accuracy, bias). The authors invoke Laurillard's (2008) technology-
enhanced pedagogy framework: **generative AI should enhance, not
replace, traditional teaching methods.** Teachers need professional
training on (1) ChatGPT usage and (2) effective classroom adoption
(Selwyn 2022), coupled with emphasis on critical AI literacy
(Mills et al. 2023; Holmes et al. 2019).

enjoyable lessons; self-reported learning correlated with interest.

**Jeon and Lee (2023).** Purpose: Korean elementary teachers'
perceptions of ChatGPT as teaching assistant. Experienced K-12
teachers; South Korea. Qualitative. Findings: ChatGPT valued for
content development and assessment assistance.

**Kakhki et al. (2024).** Purpose: affordances and risks of ChatGPT
in higher education. University students, faculty, administrators;
United States. Qualitative (grounded theory; panel sessions).
Findings: personalized self-directed learning paths; concerns about
academic integrity.

**Kortemeyer (2023).** Purpose: ChatGPT's performance on
introductory physics. Geographic context not clearly specified.
Mixed methods. Findings: ChatGPT can barely pass an introductory
physics course.

**Lee et al. (2024).** Purpose: effectiveness of a guidance-based
ChatGPT-assisted Learning Aid (GCLA) tool that engages students in
critical thinking before receiving ChatGPT responses. University
students; Taiwan. Quantitative — **RCT** (the only study in the
corpus explicitly described as a Randomized Controlled Trial).
ANCOVA. Findings: support for the GCLA approach in promoting
independent problem solving.

**Lee and Zhai (2024).** Purpose: ChatGPT support for pre-service
teachers' differentiated instruction and formative assessment
design. South Korea. Mixed methods; grounded theory + descriptive
statistics.

**Mahapatra (2024).** Purpose: ChatGPT's impact on undergraduate
academic writing. India. Quasi-experimental mixed methods
(phronetic iterative qualitative + inferential statistics).
Findings: personalized feedback increased autonomy.

**Mizumoto and Eguchi (2023).** Purpose: ChatGPT's reliability for
auto-grading L2 essays. Geographic context not clearly specified.
Quantitative; psychometric (reliability). Findings: ChatGPT
consistently graded essays aligned with linguistic criteria;
timely feedback; reduced grading time.

**Mogavi et al. (2024).** Purpose: public perceptions of ChatGPT
from social media. Public group; global. Qualitative thematic
analysis. Findings: efficiency emphasized as major benefit.

**Nikolic et al. (2023).** Purpose: SWOT analysis of ChatGPT in
engineering education and assessment. Australia. Miscellaneous
(SWOT). Findings: real-time feedback strength; assessment
vulnerability.

**Perkins et al. (2024).** Purpose: effectiveness of Turnitin's
AI-detection. University faculty; Southeast Asia. Mixed methods.
Findings: generally effective; limitations on paraphrased content.

**Rakap (2024).** Purpose: ChatGPT for Individualized Education
Program (IEP) design. Pre-service K-12 teachers. Quantitative
(experimental); inter-rater reliability with chi-square and
Pearson correlation.

**Stojanov (2023).** Purpose: autoethnographic exploration of
ChatGPT as personal learning assistant. Qualitative
autoethnography. Findings: ChatGPT useful as judgment-free tutor;
inaccurate-content risk.

**Sullivan et al. (2023).** Purpose: news-article analysis of
ChatGPT in education across NZ / Australia / UK / US. Public group;
global. Qualitative thematic + sentiment analysis.

**Taktak et al. (2024).** Purpose: SWOT of ChatGPT in K-12 from
teachers and principals. Turkey. Qualitative SWOT.

**Tlili et al. (2023).** Purpose: ChatGPT in education — public
discourse and case studies. Public; global. Qualitative case study;
thematic analysis. Findings: bias, accuracy, plagiarism flagged.

**Yan (2023).** Purpose: ChatGPT in EFL writing. University
undergraduates; China. Multi-method qualitative; thematic +
document analysis. Findings: ChatGPT supports language acquisition
with caveats on accuracy.

**Yeadon et al. (2023).** Purpose: ChatGPT's performance on physics
essay assessment. University; UK. Mixed methods. Findings: ChatGPT
can produce passing essays — assessment-design implications.

**Zhu et al. (2023).** Purpose: SWOT of ChatGPT in teaching and
learning. Miscellaneous (SWOT). Findings: relies on prior
published studies; data collection unclear.
