**[22:30]**

**Speaker 1:** Let me lay out where I am. You have a 14 on the PHQ-9, an above-threshold sub-syndromal score on the PCL-5 with the avoidance cluster doing most of the work, a mild GAD-7, normal DES. You have a clear precipitant — your father's sudden death eleven weeks ago. You have a meaningful regret-loop around an unresolved conflict in the months before he died. You have a long-standing pattern, by your own report and by what I'm gathering from Dr. Reyes's framing, of emotional containment that's now under strain. Sleep is the most concrete symptom — five hours a night, every night, for two months, is below what most adults sustain without other things degrading.

**Speaker 2:** Yeah.

**Speaker 1:** So — three questions on the table. First, is this a major depressive episode, or is this complicated grief — *prolonged grief disorder*, which is a separate DSM-5-TR diagnosis since 2022 — or is it both? Second, does medication help here, and if so, what for? Third, what's the follow-up cadence going to be?

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** On the first question — eleven weeks out, you're inside the timeframe where most clinicians would *not* call it prolonged grief disorder yet; PGD requires the disturbance to persist at least 12 months in adults. So technically not a PGD diagnosis today. The PCL-5 avoidance signal and the regret-loop are *risk factors* for moving toward PGD if the pattern hardens. That's a reason to be doing exactly what you're doing with Dr. Reyes. And the PHQ-9 of 14 is real, but in bereavement context I'd resist calling it primary MDD without more time — the DSM-5-TR removed the bereavement exclusion in 2013, but that doesn't mean every PHQ-9 above 10 in a grieving person is MDD. So what I'd write in the chart today is "bereavement-related depressive episode with complicated-grief features, PTSD avoidance cluster sub-threshold." Conservative framing on purpose.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** On the second question — medication. Dr. Reyes mentioned to me that you have a history-of-Dad's-meds reluctance, which I respect. Let me separate this into two pieces.

**Speaker 1:** Piece one: an SSRI for the depressive / grief picture as a whole. My honest read is — at eleven weeks, I'd hold off. Not because you wouldn't tolerate it; you almost certainly would. But because the work you're doing in therapy is exactly the work that, if it lands, makes the medication question moot, and the work might land *better* without the affective flattening some SSRIs introduce in the first six weeks. Grief is informational — the feelings carry information about what mattered, what's unfinished, what to do next. SSRIs can be helpful when grief becomes a closed-loop trap, but you're not in a closed loop yet — you're in active processing.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Piece two: the sleep. This is the part I'd actually consider treating separately. Five hours, every night, for two months — that's a separate problem, and it's making everything else worse. If we don't fix the sleep, we don't really know how much of the depressive symptom load is *grief* and how much is *sleep deprivation pretending to be depression*. Two options I'd consider, both small and reversible: one is a short course of trazodone, low dose, off-label, helps with sleep maintenance not just sleep onset — what your symptom pattern suggests you need. Two is a CBT-I (cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia) referral, which is the gold standard but takes weeks to land. My instinct, given how tired you are right now, is to do both — trazodone in the short term to actually get sleep, CBT-I in the medium term so we can taper off the trazodone in eight to twelve weeks.

**Speaker 2:** Hmm.

**Speaker 1:** But — and this matters — I'm not going to start the trazodone today. I want you to think about it. Talk to Sarah, talk to Dr. Reyes. I want to see you again in two weeks — let's say May 7th — by which time you'll have had two more therapy sessions and you'll have had time to sit with whether you actually want anything from me. If on May 7th you're still where you are tonight, we start the trazodone then. If between now and then the sleep gets meaningfully worse, my service will fit you in sooner. **Speaker 2:** Okay. That — yeah, that works. That actually really works.

**Speaker 1:** And the SSRI question — I'd revisit at twelve weeks, which is mid-July. The marker I'd watch is whether the avoidance cluster is loosening. If you're still avoiding your mom's calls and still avoiding talking to Sarah about how you actually are in July, that's a sign the depressive piece is consolidating into something that medication might help with. If by then the avoidance has softened — even partially — the medication question may not need to be on the table at all.

**[33:18]**

**Speaker 1:** Statistically — about 10 to 15 percent of bereaved people develop something that looks like what you're describing. Your risk factors — the unresolved fight, the long-standing containment pattern, the way the avoidance is showing up in the PCL-5 — those are reasons you'd be on the higher end of the risk band. They're also exactly what therapy with Dr. Reyes is positioned to address. That's why I'm not in a rush to medicate.
