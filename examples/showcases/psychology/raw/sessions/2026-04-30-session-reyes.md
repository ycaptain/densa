# Session — Mark Eldridge & Dr. Elena Reyes, LCSW

Date: 2026-04-30
Mode: in-person
Duration: 50 min
Session number: 3

Speaker 1 = Dr. Elena Reyes (therapist)
Speaker 2 = Mark Eldridge (client, self)

---

**[00:00]**

**Speaker 1:** Hi Mark. Come on in. You walked in differently today — I'm noticing. Slower. How are you starting?

**Speaker 2:** *(half-smile)* Han said something similar last Thursday. Maybe I'm just tired. I had a — yeah, I had a heavy week.

**Speaker 1:** Tell me about Han first. What's the takeaway you walked out with?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* He didn't prescribe. He talked about — he called it a "watchful position." He said the sleep thing — the 3:40 — was real, but he wanted to see what changed over the next two weeks before pulling the medication lever. He gave me one PRN pill for nights I really couldn't sit with it. I haven't used it.

**Speaker 1:** How did that land?

**Speaker 2:** Honestly? Better than I expected. I went in braced for a script and a fifteen-minute version of "let's see how you do on it." Instead he, um, he asked about Dad's medications. In a real way. He let me — *(pause)* — he let me talk about the blood pressure stuff Dad stopped taking. I haven't told anyone the actual story of that. He wrote it down. He said something like — *(scrolls phone notes)* — "your hesitation about medication is part of your grief, not separate from it." Which felt — yeah. That felt right.

**Speaker 1:** Did you tell him what we did in here on the 16th?

**Speaker 2:** Yeah. I told him about the foreman. He asked me to draw it for him on a piece of paper, can you believe it — a psychiatrist with a notebook and a Sharpie. I drew a stick figure with rolled-up sleeves sitting on a bucket. He kept the page.

**Speaker 1:** Good. That's — that means he and I are running compatible models for you, which is what we want.

**[04:20]**

**Speaker 1:** Okay. Before we go anywhere else — how's the chest band? Where is the hand today?

**Speaker 2:** *(touches collarbone area)* Still here. Maybe a little — a little to one side today. And not as tight. More like — like the hand is resting, not pressing.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. Stay with that for a second.

**Speaker 2:** *(quiet, exhales)*

**Speaker 1:** I want to try something today that builds on what came up on the 16th. The foreman. I want us to talk to him directly — not about him. The IFS frame calls it unblending: we make a little space between the part and the rest of you, so the part can speak, and you can listen to it without becoming it. Are you up for that?

**Speaker 2:** *(longer pause)* What does that actually look like?

**Speaker 1:** Practically — I'm going to pull this chair over here. *(slides empty chair to face Mark)* The foreman sits in that chair. You sit in your chair. You and he are going to have a short conversation. I'll keep us in bounds. You can stop any time. If at any point it feels weird or fake or silly, that's information — we'll work with it. Sound okay?

**Speaker 2:** *(skeptical, then nods)* I'll try.

**Speaker 1:** Good. Take a minute. Close your eyes if it helps. Locate the hand on your chest. And when you can see the foreman — even faintly — let me know. He doesn't have to be a full picture. A posture is fine.

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, ~30 sec, eyes closed)* Okay. He's there. Like I said — sleeves rolled up. Sitting. Looking somewhere off to the side. Not at me.

**[09:15]**

**Speaker 1:** Good. Now — open your eyes if you want, look at the empty chair. He's in it. You can ask him something. Anything. The first question that comes.

**Speaker 2:** *(opens eyes, looks at chair, long pause, ~12 sec)* I feel ridiculous.

**Speaker 1:** I know. Stay anyway.

**Speaker 2:** *(another pause, then quietly, addressing the chair)* How long have you been on shift?

**Speaker 1:** Good. Now — switch chairs.

**Speaker 2:** *(half-smile, gets up, sits in the empty chair facing his original seat)* This is so weird.

**Speaker 1:** It is. Answer as the foreman.

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, then in a slightly flatter voice)* — Since you were twelve. The summer Dad moved out the first time. Mom was on the kitchen floor for two days. You were in your room. Someone had to feed the dog and answer the door. So I picked up.

**Speaker 1:** *(quiet)* Stay with him. Ask him another.

**Speaker 2:** *(in his own voice again, looking across at the chair he just left)* — What — what do you want?

**Speaker 2 (as foreman, switching):** *(pause, ~6 sec)* I want you to keep moving. That's the whole job. Mom is on the floor again. Dad's gone for real this time. Sarah is — Sarah is doing what she can but she can't carry the house and you. The kid needs his dad. Someone has to keep moving. So I keep us moving.

**Speaker 1:** What does he need from you?

**Speaker 2 (as foreman):** *(pause)* — Not to be fired. *(longer pause)* — Not to be made fun of. I've been doing this a long time. Don't tell me to "relax." Don't tell me to "let it out." I let it out, the house falls down. I don't fucking play.

**Speaker 1:** What if he could take a break? Not be fired. Just — covered for an hour, a night, a Saturday morning?

**Speaker 2 (as foreman):** *(pause, ~10 sec)* — By who? *(this is asked sharply, with skepticism)*

**Speaker 1:** Stay with that question. You don't have to answer. Just — switch back.

**[14:45]**

**Speaker 2:** *(gets back into his original chair, exhales loudly)* That was — yeah. That was a thing.

**Speaker 1:** What did you notice while you were over there?

**Speaker 2:** I noticed I — I knew his answers before I said them. Like, I didn't have to invent anything. The skepticism — "by who" — that came out of me unbidden. And the line about my mom on the kitchen floor — I haven't thought about that summer in — I don't know. Twenty years maybe.

**Speaker 1:** That's exactly the work. The foreman isn't a metaphor I gave you. He's a configuration of you that's been running since the summer your parents separated the first time, and he carries the specific memory of why he was hired. The unblending part is — when you sit in your chair and let him sit in his chair, you can hear his story without becoming his story.

**Speaker 2:** *(nods, eyes wet but not crying)*

**Speaker 1:** I want to honour what he said about being skeptical of "by who." He's not wrong to be skeptical. He's been doing the job alone for twenty-some years. The answer to "by who" can't be a slogan. It has to be — accumulating evidence that the system can hold without him for short stretches. The Saturday call last week with your mom — twenty minutes, you held it, you sat on the porch — was *that*. Sarah brought you a beer, you didn't have to ask. The system held for an hour. The foreman saw that.

**Speaker 2:** Hm. *(pause)* That's — yeah. I didn't connect those.

**Speaker 1:** That's why we're connecting them now.

**[20:00]**

**Speaker 1:** One more move and then we're done with the chair-work for today. Sit forward. Look at the foreman in the empty chair. Tell him something.

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, ~8 sec, looking at the chair)* I — *(another pause)* — I see what you've been doing. *(longer pause, voice tightens)* — I'm not firing you. I'm not. Don't — don't worry about that.

**Speaker 1:** Take a breath.

**Speaker 2:** *(breath)* — But I — I want to start to know when you're working overtime. So I can — I don't know, so I can let you sit on the bucket for an hour. I don't know how yet. But I want to.

**Speaker 1:** Stay there. Let that land for him.

**Speaker 2:** *(~10 sec, looking at the chair, doesn't speak)*

**Speaker 1:** What's the chest doing?

**Speaker 2:** *(touches chest)* — Loose. The hand is — open. Resting on my chest, not gripping it.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah. That's exactly what we were after. Don't worry about losing it after you leave the room — it won't last all week. But the foreman now has a different piece of evidence than he had when you walked in. He's heard from you directly. That's not nothing.

**[25:30]**

**Speaker 1:** Let me step back. What just happened — in clinician language — is we did an IFS unblending exercise. You located the protector part, you let it speak, you noticed its developmental anchor at twelve, and you communicated to it without trying to dissolve it or override it. The technique is straightforward; the work is in the listening. We won't do this every session — it's a tool, not a routine — but the foreman now exists as someone you can address. That changes the geometry of everything else.

**Speaker 2:** *(quiet)* Yeah.

**Speaker 1:** Before we close — how's Sarah?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* Better than the Wednesday I told you about two weeks ago. We had — we had a thing on Sunday where I told her about my dad and the blood-pressure meds. Same thing I told Han, actually. She — she just listened. She didn't try to fix it. We sat on the couch for a long time, just — sitting. That was good.

**Speaker 1:** That's the second piece of evidence the system has accumulated this week. The first was your mom on Saturday two weeks ago. The second was Sarah on Sunday. The foreman is collecting a portfolio.

**Speaker 2:** *(half-smile)*

**Speaker 1:** And Theo?

**Speaker 2:** Theo's — Theo is being Theo. He drew a picture last weekend that I — I don't know what to do with it. He drew the family — Mom, Dad, him, and then a third figure off to the side, smaller, and when I asked who it was he said "that's Grandpa watching." I — *(pause)* — I haven't shown it to Sarah yet. I will. I just — I had to look at it for a while.

**Speaker 1:** Stay with that. We'll come back to it next time if it stays alive for you. The third figure is a thing — children grieve sideways. Their grief is concrete in a way ours stops being. We'll talk about it on the 14th.

**[31:00]**

**Speaker 1:** Two more practical things. One — between now and next session, when you notice the chest band, instead of pushing past it, see if you can do a five-second check: *foreman, what are you doing right now?* Don't expect a verbal answer. Just — acknowledge him. The unblending muscle gets stronger with low-stakes reps.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Two — call your mom on a Saturday in the next two weeks. Not because I'm prescribing it, because you said two weeks ago you wanted to. The 16th-Saturday call was a one-time stress test; what we want is to know whether a second call lands the same way or differently. Both are data.

**Speaker 2:** Okay. I think I — I think I will, actually. Maybe this Saturday.

**Speaker 1:** Don't manufacture readiness if it's not there. But also don't wait for a perfect day.

**Speaker 2:** Yeah.

**Speaker 1:** Anything you want to say before we close?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* — Just — that thing I said over there about being skeptical of "by who." I want to come back to that some week. I don't think it's just about the foreman. I think — I think Mom didn't have anyone either, that summer. And maybe that's why the foreman was so sure no one would cover for him. Because he didn't see anyone covering for her.

**Speaker 1:** *(quiet)* That's — yeah. That's a piece we'll definitely come back to. Hold it lightly until then.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Next is the 14th. Same time. Stay in your body during the week — five-second check-ins. If anything sharp happens, my service knows how to reach me.
