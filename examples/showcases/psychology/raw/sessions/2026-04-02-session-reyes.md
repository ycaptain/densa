# Session — Mark Eldridge & Dr. Elena Reyes, LCSW

Date: 2026-04-02
Mode: in-person (Reyes's office, midwest US)
Duration: 50 min
Reason for referral: self-referral, ~8 weeks after the sudden death of his father. PCP screened (PHQ-2 = 4) and suggested both psychotherapy and a psychiatry consult.

Speaker 1 = Dr. Elena Reyes (therapist)
Speaker 2 = Mark Eldridge (client, self)

---

**[00:00]**

**Speaker 1:** Mark, hi. Come in, take whichever chair feels right. The one by the window gets the afternoon sun, fair warning.

**Speaker 2:** I'll, uh, take this one. The other one feels like a hot seat. *(laughs once, short)*

**Speaker 1:** Fair. Before we start, two housekeeping things. One — what we say here stays here, the standard exceptions for safety. Two — I'll be taking light notes, mostly so I don't have to remember everything. You can ask to see them anytime. Sound okay?

**Speaker 2:** Yeah. Fine.

**Speaker 1:** Good. So your PCP, Dr. Lewis, sent me a one-line summary — your dad passed in early February and you've been having a hard time. That's about all I have. Can you walk me through what made you pick up the phone now, two months out, rather than week one or week ten?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause, ~6 sec)* Honestly I think week one I was just running on adrenaline. There was so much to do. He died on a Wednesday and by Friday I was on a plane to the funeral. I came back, and my mom was, you know, in pieces, so I went back out the next weekend, and the weekend after that. For about a month I was basically a logistics function. Probate, insurance, his apartment — he and Mom were separated, long story, he had his own place. I cleaned out his fridge by myself. I think that was the moment I knew something was wrong, actually. I was in the apartment, alone, and there's like, half a tub of cottage cheese, and I — *(pause)* I started laughing. Just laughing at this tub of cottage cheese in his fridge. And then I drove home and I was fine. So that was the first weird thing.

**Speaker 1:** *(nods, makes a brief note)* Okay. Hold onto that one — the cottage cheese — I want to come back to it. What was the second weird thing?

**Speaker 2:** Sleep. About three weeks in. I started waking up at, like, 3:40 in the morning. Not from a nightmare, not from anything. Just — eyes open, brain on. I'd lie there for an hour and a half and then get up at 5 and go to work. That's been every night for about, I think, five weeks now.

**[03:47]**

**Speaker 1:** Every night?

**Speaker 2:** Maybe one night a week I sleep through. My wife — Sarah — she sleeps next to me and she doesn't even wake up. So I'm just alone with the ceiling at 3:40.

**Speaker 1:** What are you doing during that hour and a half? Phone? Reading? Thinking?

**Speaker 2:** Mostly nothing. Just lying there. Sometimes I open my phone and then put it down because the light feels too bright. I've tried doing the breathing thing my Apple Watch suggests — that doesn't help, just makes me angrier at the watch. *(laughs)* And then around 5:00 I get up and start the day.

**Speaker 1:** And during the day?

**Speaker 2:** During the day I'm fine. I mean — I run a 14-person engineering team at — you know, I'm an engineering manager. There's a sprint, there's a roadmap, there's standup. I go to standup, I run my 1:1s, I write performance reviews. People at work, I think, don't notice anything different. My boss told me to take more time off in February and I took two weeks and then I came back and I've been at desk every day since.

**Speaker 1:** What does "fine at work" feel like from the inside?

**Speaker 2:** *(pauses ~4 sec)* Like I'm watching myself do it. Like there's a, um. There's a version of me running standup, and there's a version of me sitting six feet behind that one watching. Both of them are competent. Neither one feels like me.

**Speaker 1:** That phrase you just used — "neither one feels like me" — is that a recent thing or have you felt that way before?

**Speaker 2:** I think since about week two after he died. Maybe earlier. The first week, like I said, was logistics. Logistics felt real.

**[07:14]**

**Speaker 1:** Tell me about your dad. Whatever you want — what he was like, what you and he were like together.

**Speaker 2:** *(longer pause, ~10 sec)* He was — okay so my dad's name was Robert, Bob. He was 68 when he died. He was an electrician. Union guy. Worked at the same shop for 34 years. He had a heart attack in the lumberyard parking lot — he'd gone to buy plywood for some shelves. They said it was, um, instant. The paramedics tried for like fifteen minutes but. *(stops)*

**Speaker 1:** Take your time.

**Speaker 2:** Yeah. So that was February 4th. *(pause)* My dad and I — okay, so my dad was Vietnam-era. His dad was Korea. He grew up in a house where you didn't talk about things. He didn't talk about Vietnam, ever — he was a mechanic over there, he wasn't in combat as far as I know, but he never said the word. He never told me he loved me out loud. Not once. And before you ask — I never said it to him either. That wasn't how we were. We watched football. We worked on his truck. He helped me put a deck on the back of our house in 2019. We didn't, um. We didn't *say* things. But — I knew. And I think he knew.

**Speaker 1:** What did you know?

**Speaker 2:** That he was proud of me. That if I'd called him at 2 in the morning he'd have driven to wherever I was. That kind of thing. The fact-of-it, not the words-of-it.

**Speaker 1:** Mm.

**Speaker 2:** And so the thing that's, um. The thing that's getting me is. We had this fight about three months before he died. About my mom. I won't bore you with it but it was about who was paying for what after they separated, and he said something pretty sharp about her, and I — *(pause)* — I said something pretty sharp back. And we didn't talk for a couple of weeks. We made it up, sort of, around Christmas — we were both at my mom's for the day, that was the arrangement — but we made it up the way we did everything else, which is we just started talking about other things. Football. The truck. And so the last conversation I had with him was actually fine, it was about the playoffs, but the conversation before that one was a fight, and we never. We didn't, um. Resolve it. We just — overwrote it.

**[12:08]**

**Speaker 1:** *(quiet)* That's a lot to carry.

**Speaker 2:** I keep thinking — and I know this is, like, the most cliché bereavement thing, but — I keep thinking I should have said the words. The actual words. And I — I literally cannot picture the version of me that would have said them. So it's like, regretting something I was constitutionally incapable of doing in the first place. Which is a weird shape of regret.

**Speaker 1:** What does it feel like in your body when you say that?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* Tight. Right here. *(gestures to upper chest / lower throat)*

**Speaker 1:** Tight how?

**Speaker 2:** Like — like a hand closing around the bottom of my throat. From the inside. It's been there pretty much constantly since, I don't know, mid-February. I went to my GP because I thought it was cardiac. He ran an EKG and a stress test and everything was fine. He said it was probably anxiety. He gave me the form for the depression questionnaire, the P.H. cue 9, I think it's called, I scored a 14, and that's how I ended up at the consult with — you mentioned a Dr. Han? — next Thursday I think.

**Speaker 1:** Yeah, Dr. David Han. He's good. Conservative, careful — he won't push medication on you in the first session, he'll listen first. So you'll see him on the 23rd. That works in our favour, actually, because it gives us two or three sessions together first to map what's going on.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Let me ask — and you don't have to answer in detail today, this is just to start the chart — any thoughts of harming yourself? Suicidal thoughts, even passive ones?

**Speaker 2:** No. Honestly no. I don't — I don't want to die. I have a kid. I have Theo, he's seven. The idea is not on the table. I'm not depressed in the I-want-to-disappear way. I'm depressed in the I-can't-feel-anything way.

**Speaker 1:** That's an important distinction. Thank you. We'll keep checking in on it but I hear you.

**[17:22]**

**Speaker 1:** Tell me about your mom. You mentioned the fight with your dad was about her. And earlier you said she was "in pieces" after he died, even though they'd been separated. What's it like with her now?

**Speaker 2:** *(visibly tenses, shifts in chair)* Um. So my mom — Margaret — she lives about three hours from us. After Dad died she had a really hard six weeks. Crying a lot. Not eating well. She has a sister who lives nearby and that helps. The thing is — I should be calling her. I know I should be calling her. And I'm not. I'm — I haven't called her in like ten days. She calls me, I let it go to voicemail and I tell myself I'll call back tonight, and then I, um. I don't.

**Speaker 1:** What stops you?

**Speaker 2:** I — *(pause, longer than before, ~8 sec)* — I think — when she cries I have no idea what to do with it. Like the words don't exist. I can hold it for the first three minutes and then it's like every muscle in my face is trying to be the right face and I can't. And then I get off the phone and I'm wrecked for the rest of the day. And there's also — and this is going to sound bad — there's also the thing where if I talk to her then I have to *be* grieving with her, and right now I'm doing this thing where I'm just kind of like, going to work, putting Theo to bed, watching dumb TV with Sarah for an hour, sleeping till 3:40, and that *works*. It's a system. Talking to my mom breaks the system.

**Speaker 1:** So avoiding the call is, in a sense, protecting the system.

**Speaker 2:** Yes. Exactly. Even though I know it's bad for her. And — Sarah keeps asking, gently, "have you called your mom?" And I keep saying "tonight" and not doing it. I think Sarah's worried.

**Speaker 1:** Have you and Sarah talked about that? About what's going on for you?

**Speaker 2:** *(pause)* Some. Not really. Sarah's been great. She handled the kid, she handled the school, she handled meals for like six weeks. But I don't — I don't think I've actually told her how I am. I've told her *that* I'm not great. I haven't told her *how* I'm not great. Because I don't quite have the words and because every time I try to start, I feel like I'm asking her to take care of me on top of everything else.

**Speaker 1:** Hm. So Sarah's not getting access to the inside of it either.

**Speaker 2:** No.

**Speaker 1:** And neither was your dad. Sorry — let me make sure I have that right. Your relationship with your dad, the way you've described it, was close but unspoken. Your relationship with your mom is loving but you can't be around her grief because you have no words for it. Your relationship with Sarah is solid but you're not letting her see the inside. Is that a fair description?

**Speaker 2:** *(long pause, ~12 sec)* Yes. *(quieter)* Yes, that's fair.

**[26:40]**

**Speaker 1:** Let me sketch some structure for what I'm hearing, and you tell me if I'm off. There seem to be at least three layers. The proximal one is the grief itself — your dad died eight weeks ago. That's a fresh wound. The middle layer is the regret, the unresolved fight, the unsaid things. That's a particular flavour of grief that doesn't go away on a normal grief timeline because the closure piece is missing. The deepest layer is what looks like a long-running pattern of — and I'll use a generic phrase here and we can refine it later — *emotional containment*. You come from a line of men who didn't talk about feelings, who showed love through reliable presence and work. You learned that, you're good at it, it served you. And now your dad's death is forcing material into your system that the container wasn't designed for.

**Speaker 2:** *(nods slowly)*

**Speaker 1:** And what I'm watching you do, in your work life and with Sarah and especially with your mom, is run the old containment routine on the new material. And the new material doesn't fit. So you wake at 3:40, your throat tightens, you laugh at cottage cheese.

**Speaker 2:** Yeah.

**Speaker 1:** I want to be really careful here — I'm not telling you the routine is bad. It got you through the funeral, it got you to your mom's door three weekends in a row, it's getting you through standup right now. The routine is the part of you that's protecting you. We're going to need it. We're just going to also need other things alongside it, eventually.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Two things I want to flag before we close out today. One — the 3:40 a.m. waking, the chest tightness, the score of 14 on the PHQ — those are reasons Dr. Han is worth seeing on the 23rd. He'll look at whether medication makes sense, especially for the sleep piece. I have no skin in that game; that's his call with you. Two — between now and our next session, I'm not going to ask you to call your mom. That's too big a lift. What I'd like instead is for you to notice, once or twice this week, what happens in your body when you *think* about calling her. Just notice. Don't change anything. Write a sentence down if you can.

**Speaker 2:** Okay. I can do that.

**Speaker 1:** And — you mentioned the cottage cheese. I want you to know I noticed that. There's something in there. We'll come back to it.

**[34:18]**

**Speaker 2:** Can I ask you something?

**Speaker 1:** Of course.

**Speaker 2:** How long is this going to last.

**Speaker 1:** Honestly? I don't know. Grief isn't a stage process — that five-stages thing is a misreading of Kübler-Ross, she was writing about people facing their own deaths, not survivors. What I can tell you is that two months out, what you're describing is in normal range for a sudden bereavement combined with an unresolved relational thread. The worry would be six, nine months out and the same intensity. Right now, the worry is more about whether the *pattern* hardens into the default — and that's what we work on here.

**Speaker 2:** Okay.

**Speaker 1:** Same time next week? Two Thursdays from now, the 16th?

**Speaker 2:** Yeah. Thursday's good. Mornings before work, ideally.

**Speaker 1:** 8 a.m. works. Done. And — Mark? You did a good job today.

**Speaker 2:** Thanks.
