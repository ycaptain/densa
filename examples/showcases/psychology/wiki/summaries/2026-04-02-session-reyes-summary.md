## Narrative reconstruction

Mark arrives eight weeks post-bereavement having spent the first
month in *logistics mode* — funeral, probate, three weekends with
mother, cleaning out his father's apartment. The symptoms
emerged ~three weeks after the death, the chest band and the
3:40 a.m. wakings appearing roughly together. He went to his
PCP because the chest band felt cardiac; the cardiac work-up was
negative; the PHQ-9 administration produced a score of 14 and
the referral pathway opened.

The session's emotional arc is non-melodramatic: Mark talks in
the register Reyes calibrates to — competent, descriptive, with
occasional self-deprecation, no extended crying. The most
affectively loaded moment is the description of the unresolved
fight with his father about his mother in the months before
death ([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [07:14]) — he names
"regretting something I was constitutionally incapable of
doing in the first place" and the chest band tightens visibly.

Reyes's frame-setting is the work of the session. By 26 minutes
in she has assembled a **three-layer formulation**:

1. *Proximal*: a fresh bereavement, 8 weeks out, well within
   normal-grief timeframe.
2. *Middle*: the unresolved fight, the regret-loop, the unsaid
   things — *"a particular flavour of grief that doesn't go away
   on a normal grief timeline because the closure piece is
   missing"*.
3. *Deep*: a long-running emotional-containment style inherited
   from the paternal lineage — *"you come from a line of men who
   didn't talk about feelings, who showed love through reliable
   presence and work."*

Reyes immediately reframes the containment style as **not the
enemy** — it got Mark through the funeral, through three
weekends with his mother, through standup that morning. The
clinical move from here will be *with* the containment, not
against it.

The session ends with **two between-session instructions**, both
deliberately minimal:

- *Notice* (not change) what happens in your body when you think
  about calling your mother. Write a sentence down if you can.
- *Do not* try to call your mother this week. The protector is
  too active to push.

Reyes also signposts the psychiatry consult coming on 04-23
([[medication-arc]]) and explicitly notes that *"the worry is
more about whether the pattern hardens into the default — and
that's what we work on here."* The 6-9-month timeframe she
references aligns with the [[complicated-grief]] PGD
risk-trajectory window: by month 6, if the pattern has not
softened, the case has consolidated.

## Working formulation

*(biopsychosocial-4P lens)*

The four causal layers are populated unevenly, which is
expected at intake. **Predisposing**: multi-generational
containment-style attachment in the paternal lineage; baseline
borderline high BP; maternal-aunt depression history
(no formal childhood mental-health history for client).
**Precipitating**: sudden cardiac death of father 2026-02-04
plus the unresolved pre-death fight; the *combination* is the
precipitant, not the death alone — the regret-loop is the
characteristic feature distinguishing a complicated-grief
trajectory from a normal-grief trajectory at this timeframe.
**Perpetuating**: the [[avoidant-mother-contact]] loop is the
single most active perpetuator; [[automaton-work-mode]]
provides daily reinforcement (work goes fine, no corrective
feedback). **Protective**: intact partner (Sarah), active
parenting (Theo, 7), reliable work role, no substance
escalation beyond ~2 beers/evening, ability to seek help
(client self-referred via PCP).

The **alone-test** ([[exiles-and-firefighters]]) applies to the
decision-avoidance ("I'll call tonight" → not calling)
material: Mark's avoidance is **specifically context-bound** —
it does not appear when he is alone doing solitary tasks, only
when interpersonal contact requires affect co-regulation he
does not feel he has bandwidth for. This favours attachment /
schema attribution rather than neurodevelopmental executive
dysfunction; ADHD / possible-ASD-features are **not** flagged in
`diagnostic_signals` despite the surface appearance of "task
not getting done."

*(attachment-theory lens)*

The picture is *dismissing* in adult-attachment terms (Mikulincer
& Shaver mapping of Ainsworth's avoidant infant pattern).
Markers: minimisation of attachment needs ("we didn't say
things... but I knew"); discomfort with explicit affective
expression in attachment figures (the mother's crying, Sarah's
worry); preference for **co-regulation by parallel competence**
(working on the truck, putting up a deck together) rather than
**co-regulation by affect sharing**. The dismissing pattern is
inherited rather than self-generated — Mark explicitly traces
it through three generations
([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [07:14]). The clinical
implication is that the pattern is **constitutionally
load-bearing**, not a learned behaviour amenable to
straightforward behavioural intervention; meaningful change
requires the attachment substrate to be addressed in the
room, not lectured into modification. Reyes's choice to refuse
the "how long will this last" reassurance
([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [34:18]) is itself an
attachment-style intervention — modelling honest
co-regulation in a register Mark's lineage did not provide.

*(existential lens)*

The session's load-bearing existential thread is **regret without
the possibility of repair**. Mark articulates the shape clearly:
*"regretting something I was constitutionally incapable of doing
in the first place. Which is a weird shape of regret."*
([[2026-04-02-session-reyes]] [07:14]). This is not garden-
variety post-loss regret; it is the regret of a person whose
prior self structurally could not have done the thing now
mourned. The existential implication: the future-orientation of
meaning-making — what kind of dad Mark wants to be to Theo, what
register of speaking Mark might develop forward from here —
becomes the available work, because the past is sealed. The
session does not yet open this thread; it surfaces at session 4
([[2026-05-14-session-reyes]] [30:08]) when the protector has
allowed enough room for it.
